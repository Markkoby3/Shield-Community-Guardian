Phishing attacks trick users into revealing credentials via fake emails or websites.
Always verify the sender's email address carefully. Look for misspellings in the domain name.
Never click links in unsolicited emails — navigate directly to the website instead.
Enable two-factor authentication on all important accounts.
Report phishing attempts to the Anti-Phishing Working Group at reportphishing@apwg.org.

Data breaches occur when unauthorized parties gain access to sensitive personal or financial information.
If you suspect your data was breached, change your passwords immediately starting with email and banking.
Enable fraud alerts with the major credit bureaus: Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion.
Monitor your bank and credit card statements closely for the next 12 months.
Consider placing a credit freeze if sensitive information like your Social Security number was exposed.

Scams targeting community members often involve fake charities, lottery winnings, or impersonation of government officials.
Legitimate organizations will never ask for payment via gift cards or wire transfer.
Verify any charity before donating by checking Charity Navigator or GuideStar.
If someone claims to be from the IRS or Social Security Administration, hang up and call the official number.
Report scams to the FTC at reportfraud.ftc.gov.

Identity theft happens when someone uses your personal information without your permission.
Review your credit reports annually at AnnualCreditReport.com.
Shred documents containing personal information before discarding them.
Use strong unique passwords for every account and store them in a password manager.
Watch for unexpected bills, calls from debt collectors, or unfamiliar accounts on your credit report.

Local crime prevention requires community awareness and cooperation.
If you witness a crime in progress, call 911 immediately — do not intervene yourself.
Report suspicious activity to your local non-emergency police line.
Join or start a Neighborhood Watch program to coordinate with neighbors.
Keep your home secure: lock doors and windows, use exterior lighting, and avoid advertising vacations on social media.

Infrastructure outages including power, water, and internet can affect community safety.
Keep a 72-hour emergency supply kit with water, food, medications, and a battery-powered radio.
Know your local emergency shelter locations in case of extended outages.
Sign up for your local government's emergency alert system for real-time notifications.
During power outages, never use generators or camp stoves indoors due to carbon monoxide risk.

Cybersecurity best practices for residents and small businesses.
Keep all software and operating systems updated — most breaches exploit known vulnerabilities.
Use a VPN when connecting to public Wi-Fi networks.
Back up important data using the 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, on 2 different media, with 1 offsite.
Be cautious about what personal information you share on social media.
